Emergency Scripts

Ready-to-use responses for the moments that catch you off guard.

Section A: Homework Unstuck

For when your child hits a wall. These prompts are for your child to paste directly into any AI tool — they get AI back into tutor mode without doing the work for them.

Child pastes into AI

🤔 I'm Stuck — Give Me a Hint

When to use: Child has tried something but can't get past a specific step 
I'm stuck and I've tried [describe what you tried]. Can you give me a clue about the next step without solving it for me?
Child pastes into AI

📖 Show Me One Example

When to use: Child understands the concept but can't execute it 
I've tried these steps and I'm still confused. Can you show me ONE worked example of a similar problem — not this exact one — and then give me a new problem to try on my own?
Child pastes into AI

🎮 Explain It Differently

When to use: The standard explanation just isn't clicking 
I've read the explanation twice and it's not clicking. Can you explain [concept] using an analogy related to [basketball/gaming/cooking/music — whatever your kid is into]? Then quiz me to see if I got it.
Child pastes into AI

🧩 This Feels Overwhelming

When to use: The task feels too big to start 
This feels like too much at once. Can you break this into the 3 smallest possible first steps and just help me with step one?
Child pastes into AI

✅ Quiz Me

When to use: Child thinks they understand — time to verify 
I think I understand this now. Can you quiz me with 3 questions to test if I actually get it? If I get one wrong, don't just tell me the answer — ask me a question to help me figure out where my thinking went wrong.

Section B: School Flag Response

For when a teacher flags your child's work. Use these to buy time, gather evidence, and respond calmly — in this order.

Parent sends to teacher

📧 Responding to a Flag — First Email

When to use: The moment you hear from the teacher — sends immediately, buys time
Subject: Re: [Assignment Name] — Request to Review Process
Dear [Teacher Name],

Thank you for bringing this to my attention. I want to work through this with you and I appreciate you reaching out directly.

[Child's name] did use AI tools on this assignment, but as a tutor — not as the writer. We've been working on a documentation system at home, and I have chat logs and drafts that show the full process.

Would you be available for a brief call or meeting this week? I'd love to walk you through the documentation together. I'm confident we can resolve this quickly.

Thank you for your patience.

[Your name]
Parent says in meeting

🗣️ Meeting Opening — Parent to Teacher

When to use: Opening line for the in-person or phone meeting — sets a collaborative tone 
"Thank you for meeting with me. I want to start by saying I understand why this raised a flag, and I'm not here to be defensive. [Child's name] did use AI, but we've been teaching them to document their process as they go. I have the chat logs here — would it help to look through them together? I want to make sure you can see the thinking behind the work."
Parent presents in meeting

📋 Presenting the Documentation

When to use: Once the teacher is willing to review — walk through the evidence in this order 
"Here's what we have:

[1] The initial prompt that shows what [child] asked the AI to do — you can see they asked it to ask them questions, not write for them.

[2] The conversation log showing the AI's questions and [child]'s answers.

[3] Three drafts showing how the essay evolved.

[4] The completed AI Partnership Disclosure form.

Is there anything else that would be helpful?"

Section C: AI Safety Concern

For when AI says something it shouldn't — or when you discover something concerning. Stay calm. These scripts keep the door open.

Parent says to child

💬 Starting the Safety Conversation

When to use: You want to open this topic proactively, before anything has happened 
"Hey — I want to talk about something, and I promise this isn't a lecture. I read that some AI tools can say things that are really off sometimes — weird advice, dark stuff, things that push people in directions that aren't healthy. Has that ever happened to you? It's not a red flag if it has. I just want to know so we can talk about it."
Parent says to child

🤝 If They Tell You Something Happened

When to use: They disclose something concerning — your response in this moment sets the tone for everything after 
"Thank you for telling me. I mean it — that took guts. I'm not upset with you at all. [Pause.] Can you show me what happened so I understand exactly what we're dealing with? We're going to figure this out together. You're not in trouble."
Parent action guide

🚨 Reporting Concerning AI Content — Step by Step

When to use: You've found content that crossed a line — screenshot first, then follow these steps 
Step 1: Screenshot the conversation immediately — before reporting or closing anything. Platform reports don't always preserve the conversation.

Step 2: Find the Report button. Most platforms (Character.AI, Snapchat, ChatGPT) have a "Report" or "Flag" button on individual messages. Tap or click the message, then look for a flag or three-dot menu.

Step 3: Select the most specific category available — e.g., "harmful content" or "inappropriate for minors." Vague reports are deprioritized.

Step 4: Save your screenshot separately to your phone's camera roll or a folder on your computer. Do this even after reporting.

Step 5: If the content involves self-harm or safety threats, contact the platform's Trust & Safety team directly via email in addition to the in-app report. Don't wait for an automated response.

Conversation Guide

Scripts and battle cards for starting — and surviving — the AI conversation with your kids.

📋 How to Use This Tab

1
Before the conversation

Read the opening script below. Copy it, practice it out loud, make it yours — it doesn't need to be verbatim.

2
During the conversation

If things go sideways, use the filters to find your situation fast. Click any card to expand the full script.

3
For next time

Print the Battle Cards and keep them somewhere handy — you won't always have time to open a browser mid-conversation.

📌 Start Here — The One Thing to Say First

Before any script, any rule, any conversation about what AI can and can't do — say this to your child. Or something like it, in your own words.

"I want you to be really successful as an adult. Not just get good grades — actually successful. Someone who can think through hard problems, learn new things fast, figure out what's true, and make smart decisions under pressure. Someone who can pursue whatever lights you up — your passion, your path, whatever that turns out to be — and actually thrive doing it. Not because someone handed it to you, but because you have the skills to figure things out, think strategically, and use every tool available to go further than you could on your own.

AI can either help you build those skills faster, or it can quietly steal them from you while making everything look fine on the surface. A kid who uses AI to do their thinking can end up with good grades but weak skills. A kid who uses AI to push their thinking will end up with strong skills — and honestly, likely better grades too.

That's the only thing I care about here. Think of it like a calculator — a great tool, but only if you understand the math underneath it. AI is the same idea, just for thinking. Use it. But make sure the thinking is still yours."

Why this works: It removes the adversarial framing entirely. You're not a cop enforcing rules. You're a parent who wants them to win — and who sees a risk they might not see yet. That's a completely different conversation.

Battle Cards — When Teens Push Back

Filter by situation type, click a card to expand the script, copy it, and say it in your own words.

Avoidance Situation

They say "I don't know" to everything

+
What's Happening

Disengagement — they're hoping you'll give up

Your Move 
"That's okay. Let me ask it differently. If you HAD to guess, what would you say?" (Then wait. Silence is your friend here.)
Shutdown Situation

"Whatever. It's fine."

+
What's Happening

Shutdown — they feel criticized or monitored

Your Move 
"I hear you. I'm not trying to make this a big deal. I just want us to have a plan so neither of us has to stress about it. Two minutes, then we're done."
Defensiveness Situation

"Everyone uses it. I'm not doing anything wrong."

+
What's Happening

Defensiveness — they feel singled out

Your Move 
"I know, lots of people are using it! Some are using it in really smart ways that actually help them learn and grow. And some are using it in ways that could hurt them down the road, and they don't even realize it yet. I just want to make sure it's actually working for you, not against you."
Anger Situation

They get angry or storm off

+
What's Happening

Overwhelm — too much, wrong moment

Your Move 
Let them go. Wait 20 minutes. Then: "I don't need to finish this tonight. But can we pick a time this week when you're not stressed? I actually think you'll find this useful."
Testing You Situation

"You don't understand how it works."

+
What's Happening

Actually an opening — they want to explain

Your Move 
"You're probably right. Show me. I want to understand how you use it." (Then actually listen. This often becomes the best conversation.)
Avoidance Situation

They're hiding usage — you find out after

+
What's Happening

Fear of punishment drove it underground

Your Move 
"I'm not angry. I want to understand why you didn't feel like you could tell me. That part matters more to me than the AI thing." (Then address the hiding, not the AI.)
Defensiveness Situation

"It's just for fun, it's not hurting anything."

+
What's Happening

They've compartmentalized — school vs. personal

Your Move 
"I'm not worried about the fun part — honestly, a lot of what you're doing with it is really cool. But here's the thing: this technology is moving faster than anyone can keep up with, including the people building it. It doesn't have a conscience, it doesn't always get things right, and sometimes it just... agrees with everything you say whether it's true or not. So I'm not worried you're doing something wrong — I'm just curious. Has it ever said something that felt off, or weird, or like it was pushing you in a direction that didn't feel quite right?"
Shutdown Situation

They shut down and won't engage at all

+
What's Happening

Wrong environment or timing

Your Move 
Change the setting. Try in the car, on a walk, or anywhere side-by-side — research consistently shows teens open up more when they're not being looked at directly. Face-to-face can feel like an interrogation; side-by-side can feel like a conversation.
Defensiveness Situation

"My teacher said we can't use it at all."

+
What's Happening

Conflicting rules between home and school

Your Move 
"Good — then we follow that teacher's rules in that class, no exceptions. But let's talk about the other classes, and what to do when the rules aren't clear."
Testing You Situation

They turn it around — "Do YOU use AI?"

+
What's Happening

Testing fairness — a genuine question

Your Move 
"Yes, actually. I use it for [real example]. And I've made mistakes with it too. That's actually part of why I want us to talk about this together." (Honesty here earns enormous trust.)
Anger Situation

The Reset — the whole conversation has gone sideways

+
What's Happening

They've stopped hearing you — it feels like surveillance, not support

Your Move 
Stop. Take a breath. And say this:

"Let me start over. I don't actually care about the AI rules right now. What I care about is that you become an adult who genuinely thrives — who thinks strategically, follows your passions, and knows how to leverage tools to go further, rather than having tools and companies leverage you. Someone who can figure things out, learn fast, and make smart decisions on your own terms.

That's what I want for you. Everything else — the rules, the documentation, all of it — is just me trying to protect that.

So can we talk about that instead? What do you actually want? What are you passionate about, and what do you want to be really good at?"
Testing You Situation

The Over-Sharer — they tell you something that surprises or concerns you

+
What's Happening

They're actually trusting you — this determines whether they keep opening up

Your Move 
"Thank you for telling me that. Seriously — I'm glad you said something. I'm not going to freak out, I just want to understand it better. Tell me more about what happened."

Then go slow. Ask one question. Don't pile on. The goal right now is not to solve the problem — it's to make sure they walk away thinking "I can tell my parent things." That's worth more than any single conversation.
Bargaining Situation

The Bargainer — "Fine, I'll only use it for brainstorming. Can we be done now?"

+
What's Happening

Offering a quick concession to close the conversation without having it

Your Move 
"I appreciate that — and I actually believe you mean it. But I don't want a promise, I want us to actually figure this out together so you're not guessing at the rules. Five more minutes. What would feel fair to you?"
Defensiveness Situation

The Catastrophizer — "So you're saying I can NEVER use AI?"

+
What's Happening

They've heard restriction where you said guidance — a common teen distortion

Your Move 
"No — that's actually the opposite of what I'm saying. I'm not here to take anything away. AI is going to be part of your life and honestly most likely part of your career. I want you to use it. I just want to make sure you know how to use it in a way that actually makes you more capable, not less. That's it."

Age-Appropriate Conversation Starters

Pull a starter that matches your child's age and the situation you're navigating.

👶 For Kids Under 11

School & Homework

  • "I heard AI can help with math homework — have you ever tried asking it a question? What happened?"
  • "Did you know some AI tools are like really patient teachers who ask you questions instead of just giving you answers? Want to see one?"

Fun & Creative

  • "What's the coolest thing you've seen AI make? A song? A picture? Show me!"
  • "If you could have an AI help you make anything — a game, a story, anything — what would you make?"

Safety & Boundaries

  • "If a computer program ever said something weird or that made you feel funny, you'd tell me right? Even if you thought I might be upset?"
  • "Let's make a rule together: what things do we want to keep just for humans — no AI allowed?"

🧑‍🎓 For Teens (12–18)

School & Productivity

  • "When you use AI for homework, does it feel like it's actually helping you learn, or more like a shortcut to get done faster? I'm genuinely curious."
  • "Have you ever gotten a better grade on something because of AI, and then realized you didn't actually understand the topic when it came up again?"
  • "What would you do if a teacher accused you of cheating with AI on something you actually wrote yourself?"
  • "You know how in math you learned how to do it by hand before you were allowed to use a calculator? I think about AI the same way. You should absolutely use it — but do you feel like you actually understand the stuff underneath it? Or does it ever feel like you're skipping that part?"

Creative & Entertainment

  • "What AI tools are you actually using for fun right now? I want to know what's actually interesting to you — not a lecture, I promise."
  • "Do you ever feel like the content you get served (videos, music, whatever) is kind of... designed to keep you watching? Does that feel weird?"

Emotional & Companionship

  • "I heard that some kids talk to AI when they're stressed or need someone to talk to. Does that ever happen for you? What's that like?"
  • "Has AI ever said something to you that felt off, or like it was pushing you in a direction that didn't feel right?"

Autonomy & Future

  • "Honestly — do you feel like you understand how these tools work well enough to be in control of them? Or does it ever feel the other way around?"
  • "What do you think AI is going to change about the kind of work you want to do someday?"

School Liaison

Email templates and advocacy scripts for every school situation — from proactive check-ins to plagiarism disputes.

📋 How to Use These Templates

1
Fill in the brackets

2
Send Template 1 now — don't wait

The proactive email sent at the start of the semester prevents 90% of future problems. Most situations never need to go past Template 2.

3

If an issue arises, use Templates 2 → 3 → 4 in sequence. Each one is calibrated for a specific level of urgency. Jump straight to 4 only as a last resort.

Template 1 of 5 Proactive — Send at Start of Semester

Proactive AI Policy Inquiry

When to use: Before any issue arises. Send to each of your child's core teachers at the start of the year or semester.
Subject: Question About AI Usage Guidelines for [Student Name]'s Class
Dear [Teacher Name],

I hope this email finds you well. As AI tools become more integrated into daily life, I want to make sure [Student Name] is using them appropriately for your class.

I know many schools are still developing their policies around AI, and I want to support whatever approach you prefer. Could you help me understand:

• Are there specific AI tools you encourage or discourage for homework?
• How would you like students to document AI usage if they use it as a study tool?
• Are there certain assignments where AI should not be used at all?

My goal is to help [Student Name] use AI as a learning tool — like a tutor — rather than a shortcut. If you have any resources or guidelines you can share, I would appreciate it.

Thank you for your time, and I look forward to partnering with you this year!

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 2 of 5 Defensive — AI Detector Flag

The False Flag Rebuttal

When to use: Your child's work was flagged by an AI detector and you have documentation proving their process.
Subject: Re: [Assignment Name] — Process Documentation Attached
Dear [Teacher Name],

Thank you for reaching out about [Student Name]'s [assignment name]. I appreciate you bringing this to my attention directly, and I want to work through this with you.

[Student Name] did use AI tools while working on this assignment — but as a tutor, not as the writer. We've been teaching them to document their AI use as they go, and I have the following materials available:

• Chat logs showing the AI asked [Student Name] questions rather than providing answers
• Three draft versions showing how the work evolved over time
• A completed AI Partnership Disclosure form explaining exactly what AI was used for

I'd welcome the opportunity to review these together at your convenience — in person, by phone, or over email. My goal is simply to show you the thinking process behind the work.

If [Student Name] needs to demonstrate their understanding in an additional way — a verbal explanation, a follow-up assignment, or anything else — we're completely open to that. We want you to feel confident in their work.

Thank you for your patience and for being willing to look at this more closely.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Contact Information]
Template 3 of 5 Accommodation — IEP / 504

IEP/504 AI Accommodation Request

When to use: Your child has an IEP or 504 plan and you want AI tools added as an official accommodation at the next meeting.
Subject: Request to Add AI Assistive Technology to [Student Name]'s [IEP/504 Plan]
Dear [IEP Coordinator / Case Manager Name],

I'd like to request that we add language to [Student Name]'s [IEP/504 Plan] at our next meeting to formally include AI assistive technology as an accommodation.

As you know, [Student Name] has [briefly state disability], which impacts their ability to [specific challenge: organize thoughts, write fluently, access complex texts, etc.]. We've found that AI tools function similarly to accommodations already in place — like text-to-speech and graphic organizers — but provide more independence and efficiency.

I'd like to propose the following accommodation language:

"[Student Name] is permitted to use AI tools including ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Grammarly, and similar platforms for brainstorming, organizational support, grammar checking, and concept clarification to mitigate the impact of [specific disability]. All final work products must reflect [Student Name]'s original thinking and writing. [Student Name] will document AI use as part of their process when required by teachers."

I'm happy to discuss this further and to share research on how other districts are implementing AI accommodations. Thank you for considering this request.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 4 of 5 Escalation — Last Resort

Admin Escalation

When to use: You've provided documentation (Template 2) and the teacher has been unreasonable. Involve administration only after exhausting direct options.
Subject: Request for Meeting Re: [Student Name] and AI Use Policy
Dear [Principal / Assistant Principal Name],

I'm reaching out because I've hit an impasse with [Teacher Name] regarding [Student Name]'s [assignment name], and I'm hoping you can help us find a fair resolution.

Background: [Student Name]'s work was flagged for AI use on [date]. I responded on [date] with process documentation — including chat logs, multiple drafts, and a completed AI Partnership Disclosure form — demonstrating that [Student Name] used AI as a learning tool, not as a replacement for their own thinking. [Teacher Name] has [describe the impasse — e.g., "declined to review the documentation" / "maintained the zero grade without discussion"].

I'm not asking for special treatment — I'm asking for the same fair process I believe any student deserves: the opportunity to demonstrate their work through their documented process.

I'd welcome a meeting with you, [Teacher Name], and [Student Name] to review the documentation together and find a resolution. I believe we can get there.

Thank you for your time.

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Template 5 of 5 Proactive — Build Trust Early

Tutor-to-Teacher Bridge

When to use: You want to proactively explain the Socratic Prompting system to a teacher before it comes up as an issue. Send early in the semester for any class with major writing or project-based assignments.
Subject: Sharing Our Home AI Learning Approach for [Student Name]
Dear [Teacher Name],

I wanted to share something we've been doing at home that I think you'll find relevant to [Student Name]'s work in your class.

We've been teaching [Student Name] to use AI in "Socratic Mode" — meaning instead of asking AI for answers, they ask it to ask them questions. For example, instead of typing "solve this equation," they type "I need help with this equation — don't solve it for me, ask me questions to guide me through the steps."

The result is a conversation log that shows [Student Name]'s thinking process rather than just a final answer. We've been attaching these logs (along with a brief AI Partnership Disclosure form) to major assignments.

I wanted to make you aware of this approach in case you see [Student Name]'s work and notice elements that reflect this process. I'm happy to share an example conversation log so you can see what it looks like in practice.

Thank you for everything you do for [Student Name] this year.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
